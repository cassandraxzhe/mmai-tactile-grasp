# Introduction

Humans anticipate the tactile consequences of their actions before contact occurs. When reaching for a glass of water, a coffee mug, or a set of keys, the hand begins forming its grip configuration hundreds of milliseconds before fingers touch the object — a process driven by predictive internal models of expected tactile feedback rather than reactive correction after contact. This anticipatory behavior enables fluid, dexterous manipulation across a vast range of object geometries, weights, and surface textures. Robotic manipulation systems, by contrast, largely treat touch as a reactive signal: tactile feedback enters the control pipeline only after contact, where it is used to detect slip, adjust grip force, or trigger corrective actions [CITE]. This fundamental asymmetry — predictive in humans, reactive in robots — limits the dexterity and robustness of current robotic systems, particularly in unstructured environments where novel objects and contact configurations are common.

Recent work has begun to close the gap between vision and touch in robotic manipulation, though primarily through retrieval, classification, and reactive control rather than proactive prediction. The OpenTouch dataset [CITE] demonstrated that cross-modal retrieval between egocentric video and tactile signals is feasible, and that tactile information is highly diagnostic of grasp type. UniTouch [CITE] aligned tactile representations to pretrained multimodal embedding spaces, enabling zero-shot cross-modal transfer across sensor types. In the policy learning domain, visuo-tactile pretraining has been shown to improve dexterous manipulation success rates significantly over vision-only baselines [CITE]. However, a critical capability remains unexplored: the generative prediction of full-hand tactile signals from pre-contact visual and proprioceptive cues. No existing work treats tactile anticipation as a standalone generative world modeling problem, and the question of whether pre-contact signals carry sufficient information to predict contact outcome has not been directly addressed.

In this paper, we propose a cross-modal predictive framework that addresses this gap in two stages. First, we establish that pre-contact egocentric video and 3D hand pose trajectories are predictive of grip type — the discrete hand configuration formed at contact — using a multimodal classifier trained on the OpenTouch dataset. We treat this as an empirical precondition for the broader generative goal: if pre-contact signals cannot predict even a coarse discrete summary of contact outcome, they cannot support a full pressure map prediction model. Second, we use these findings to motivate a conditional diffusion model that generates anticipated full-hand tactile pressure maps ($16 \times 16$) from pre-contact observations, framing tactile prediction as a cross-modal world modeling problem. We ground our approach in a key empirical finding from data exploration: fingertip spread decreases monotonically in the frames leading up to contact, reflecting the hand closing into its grip preshape before any tactile feedback is received. This demonstrates that the pre-contact window contains structured, temporally coherent signals that encode grasp intent — exactly the signals our model is designed to exploit.
